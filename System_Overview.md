MATERIALS AND METHODS
	Framework Overview
This research introduces a novel multi-agent architecture that integrates IoT sensing modalities with LLM-based autonomous agents for continuous cardiovascular monitoring in ambient assisted living environments. The proposed framework implements a distributed system of specialized intelligent agents that collaborate to enable seamless clinical data acquisition, processing, and patient interaction via natural language interfaces. Designed for patient-centered care, the framework ensures reliable data collection, transparent decision support, and trustworthy recommendations that are essential for effective health management.


 
Fig. 1. Overview of the Proposed Agentic AI Framework for Clinical IoT–Based ECG Acquisition and Decision Support


As illustrated in Fig. 1, the system combines synergistic components: 
•	The Perception and Data Acquisition component forms the foundational infrastructure for comprehensive data collection from diverse sources in ambient assisted living environments, including automated sensing modalities and conversational interfaces. This component incorporates wearable and non-wearable IoT biosensors that continuously capture real-time physiological signals. In the current prototype implementation, ECG is used as the primary sensing modality to demonstrate the system’s capabilities, while the architecture remains fully extensible to additional cardiovascular sensors. A key innovation is the integration of conversational clinical data collection through natural language interfaces, where LLM-powered agents engage patients in interactive dialogues to gather subjective health information, including symptom descriptions, lifestyle factors, medication adherence, and contextual health narratives not obtainable through automated sensors alone.
•	The Intelligent Agent Orchestration represents the core architectural innovation of the proposed framework, implementing a multi-agent system where specialized LLM-based autonomous agents collaborate to manage distinct aspects of patient data acquisition and validation. Instead of a monolithic AI model, this component employs four specialized agents: the Static Data Agent manages historical clinical records and baseline patient information; the Daily Data Agent handles patient-reported daily health logs and biomarker tracking; the IoT Agent interfaces with medical devices to guide real-time biosignal collection; and the Imaging Agent uploads and validates diagnostic imaging data when applicable. 
Each agent leverages natural language processing to enable conversational interactions with patients, clarifying ambiguities, verifying data accuracy, and providing contextual guidance throughout the data collection process. The IoT Agent, in particular, acts as an intelligent intermediary between conversational AI and medical hardware, delivering step-by-step instructions to patients during ECG recording sessions while simultaneously assessing signal quality to ensure clinical-grade data acquisition. Inter-agent communication protocols facilitate workflow coordination, ensuring data completeness across all modalities before downstream analysis, thus preventing incomplete assessments that could compromise diagnostic accuracy.
•	The Processing and Analysis component follows a hybrid edge–cloud workflow designed to optimize efficiency and responsiveness. Basic signal filtering and quality enhancement are performed directly on the ECG sensor device to ensure clean and reliable data before transmission. More computationally intensive tasks such as feature extraction and pattern recognition are executed in the cloud, where software processes the prefiltered signals. 
•	The Decision Support and Reasoning component addresses the critical challenge of clinical interpretability by implementing a dual-track approach that integrates data-driven AI predictions with established medical reasoning frameworks. This component incorporates a medical risk scoring system based on well-validated clinical indices, providing trusted and standardized risk assessment mechanisms routinely used in evidence-based clinical practice. Complementing these scores, a Fuzzy Inference System (FIS) employs rule-based reasoning to emulate clinical decision-making, translating numerical measurements and uncertain clinical observations into interpretable risk categories through fuzzification, knowledge-based inference engines, and defuzzification modules. This hybrid architecture mitigates the "black-box" limitation of pure machine learning systems, ensuring that automated outputs are both statistically accurate and clinically meaningful, thereby enhancing their actionability for patients and healthcare providers.
•	The Visualization and Monitoring component provides accessible interfaces for both patients and clinicians through a secure web-based dashboard. For patients, the system offers natural language explanations of health findings, ECG waveform views, trend summaries, and personalized recommendations, supported by an integrated alert system for timely notifications. Clinicians access consolidated reports that combine multimodal data, risk scores, and decision-support tools aligned with clinical guidelines. Security features such as role-based access control, encrypted communication, and audit logging ensure regulatory compliance and protect sensitive health information.
	Proposed Framework Architecture
	ECG sensor
The ECG acquisition hardware layer is responsible for capturing and transmitting the physiological signals used in the proposed prototype. Raw ECG waveforms are recorded using the AD8232 signal acquisition module through a standard electrode configuration [17]. The module incorporates an analog front-end conditioning stage designed to mitigate common challenges in bioelectric signal acquisition, including baseline drift, ambient noise, and the low amplitude of cardiac electrical activity. As summarized in the accompanying Table 2, this conditioning relies on a multi-stage filtering pipeline that ensures stable and interpretable ECG output. The processed signals are then transmitted wirelessly via an ESP32 module using Bluetooth communication. Bluetooth was selected due to its low power consumption and adequate bandwidth for reliable ECG transmission at the target sampling frequency, enabling continuous and energy-efficient monitoring in ambient assisted living settings.

	Hardware Prototype and Edge-Computing Configuration

The physical IoMT acquisition node integrates an ESP32-WROOM-32 microcontroller with an AD8232 analog front-end (AFE) module for continuous single-lead ECG monitoring. The analog signal is digitized using the ESP32's internal 12-bit SAR ADC, configured with 11 dB attenuation (0-3.3 V full-scale range). To eliminate board-specific non-linearities, the firmware applies the ESP32 eFuse two-point factory calibration (esp_adc_cal), ensuring precise millivolt-level conversion. Sampling is strictly regulated by a microsecond-precision hardware timer, maintaining a fixed rate of 500 Hz without timing drift. (see Fig. 5).
 
Fig. 5. ECG device prototype real implementation: RL is green, LO+ is red and LO- is yellow.

Prior to wireless transmission, the digitized signal undergoes a three-stage edge-level DSP pipeline to preserve morphological fidelity and minimize backend latency. A first-order IIR high-pass filter (0.5 Hz cutoff) removes baseline wander from respiration and electrode polarization, followed by a tuned IIR biquad notch filter (50 Hz) to suppress mains interference. Subsequently, a lightweight Pan-Tompkins algorithm computes the first derivative, squares the signal, and applies a 15-sample moving-window integration to detect QRS complexes in real time. This enables on-device heart rate computation and R-peak flagging before data leaves the node.

To optimize BLE resource constraints, the firmware employs an atomic double-buffering scheme that decouples the 500 Hz sampling interrupt from the BLE transmission loop. Filtered samples are batched into 10-sample groups and transmitted via GATT notifications at 50 Hz using a strictly defined 26-byte binary protocol. Each packet contains: (1) a 16-bit rolling sequence counter for mathematical packet-loss tracking; (4) ten 16-bit signed integers representing the ECG waveform (0.1 mV resolution); (3) a hardware lead-off flag (sampled from AD8232 LO+/LO- pins at GPIO 35/34); (4) edge-computed heart rate (BPM) and a QRS detection flag; and (5) an XOR checksum over bytes 0-24 to guarantee payload integrity against RF corruption. Coupled with an expanded ATT_MTU (185 bytes), this binary telemetry architecture reduces transmission overhead by more than 80% compared to ASCII-based alternatives, providing a deterministic, low-latency data pipeline that seamlessly interfaces with the Executor middleware for downstream agentic orchestration and signal quality governance.

	The Intelligent Agent Orchestration
The proposed system employs a Multi-Agent System architecture (MAS) wherein each module operates as an LLM-driven agent. Unlike centralized AI solutions, this framework distributes intelligence across specialized conversational agents that collaboratively manage the clinical workflow. Each agent follows the ReAct (Reasoning + Acting) paradigm [18], combining natural language reasoning with tool execution to perform structured clinical tasks. Within this paradigm, agents iteratively: 
●	interpret the current context (e.g., patient state, prior messages, sensor status),
●	decompose high-level objectives into intermediate reasoning steps expressed in natural language
●	invoke tools that interface with external resources, such as querying databases, triggering ECG acquisition, validating data quality, or updating patient records. Tool outputs are then fed back into the agent’s reasoning loop, enabling corrective actions, clarification questions to the patient, or escalation to other agents when required.
The Agentic Architecture operationalizes the multi-agent layer of the system by instantiating a set of LLM-driven agents that share a common internal design, are coordinated by a dedicated orchestration middleware, and execute a deterministic clinical workflow for cardiovascular data collection and enrichment. 
The next points introduce the generic agent architecture shared by all agents, then detail the conversational orchestration mechanism, and finally describe the four specialized agents that constitute the execution pipeline.
	Agent’s Generic Architecture
All four agents in the proposed framework share a standardized structural design that promotes consistency, interoperability, and extensibility across the system, as illustrated in Fig. 2.
Each agent is instantiated as an LLM-based entity augmented with specialized behavioral constraints, tool interfaces, memory mechanisms, and real-time communication capabilities:

●	LLM core (“agent brain”): At the center of each agent lies an LLM that serves as the primary reasoning engine. Rather than operating as a static classifier, the LLM follows the ReAct (Reasoning + Acting) paradigm, interleaving natural language reasoning traces with task-specific tool invocations to decide whether to continue thinking, call an external function, or generate a response.
●	Agent behavior via prompt engineering: Each agent is guided by a system prompt that encodes its role, clinical domain, behavioral rules, and input–output formats. The prompt constrains conversational style, enforces safety policies, and integrates patient-specific context, which together shape the agent’s behavior as a domain-aware clinical assistant.
●	Binding tools: Each agent is bound to a predefined set of domain-specific tools, enabling structured interaction with patient data repositories, analytical services, and connected devices. By coupling LLM-driven reasoning with tool-mediated execution, the architecture bridges natural language interaction and operational control.
●	Memory and context persistence: Each agent maintains session-level memory that stores conversational history, intermediate outputs, and context required to sustain coherent multi-turn interactions. The State Management System is scoped to the active session to support continuity while respecting privacy constraints, and a lightweight checkpointing mechanism enables agents to resume after interruptions without re-running completed steps.
●	Error handling and resilience: To support robust operation in clinical environments, the framework incorporates orchestrator-managed fault handling, where technical failures are detected at the middleware layer and communicated to agents through structured error reports. Agents then reason over these observations and invoke fallback tools to deliver patient-friendly corrective guidance while preserving conversational context, thereby isolating low-level system faults from the user-facing interaction and maintaining reliability and trust.
●	Real-time communication via WebSocket: Each agent interacts with the orchestration layer through a dedicated WebSocket channel, providing low-latency, bidirectional communication over a persistent connection.
This protocol allows agents to receive updated context and deliver outputs in real time, while the orchestrator sequentially transfers channel control between agents as they enter and exit their execution states.
	Executor as Conversational Orchestrator
The orchestration layer is centered on the Executor, a middleware component that coordinates the multi-agent workflow and mediates interactions between LLM-based agents and connected IoT devices. Acting as the system’s control backbone, the Executor ensures coherent task execution, safe tool invocation, and seamless transitions between specialized agents while maintaining a continuous conversational experience for the patient.
The Executor fulfills three primary roles. 
●	First, it manages agent sequencing, transferring control between agents based on structured completion signals, thereby enabling modular task delegation without exposing internal role boundaries to the user. 
●	Second, it centralizes tool-mediated execution, handling all device operations and data-management tasks requested by agents. This separation of high-level reasoning from operational control simplifies verification, supports extensibility, and reduces coupling between agents and hardware. 
●	Third, the Executor provides fault normalization, monitoring device interactions and transforming technical failures into structured error reports that agents can reason over. Agents then translate these reports into patient-friendly guidance through ReAct-based fallback strategies, allowing recovery to occur within the ongoing dialogue without disrupting interaction flow.
Through these mechanisms, the Executor becomes the central coordination hub that synchronizes autonomous agents and bridges intelligent reasoning with real-world execution, while enforcing safety and reliability constraints required in clinical applications.
	LLM-Driven Clinical Data Agents
All four agents in the proposed framework share the same generic architecture described. Additionally, each agent is therefore instantiated as an LLM-based module with identical internal structure yet specialized behavior, determined by its role-specific instructions and the tools it can invoke as detailed in Table 3. Building on this shared design, the agents are executed in a deterministic sequential pipeline that follows the natural chronology of a cardiovascular assessment:
1.	Static Data Agent (SDA): retrieves and organizes baseline medical history and long-term risk factors. The SDA extracts structured data, including chronic conditions, lifestyle factors, and medical history, using an LLM to interpret unstructured clinical notes, when necessary, thereby establishing a comprehensive patient profile.
2.	Daily Data Agent (DDA): is responsible for managing time-varying clinical information that evolves over short temporal scales, including routine laboratory measurements, anthropometric parameters, and home-monitored physiological indicators. Data are acquired through structured conversational interactions or ingested from connected devices and electronic health record systems. Operating under a medically constrained prompt, the DDA enforces semantic and syntactic consistency by guiding patients to provide values with appropriate units, timing, and contextual qualifiers (e.g., fasting status). In addition, the agent offers concise, context-aware explanations of clinical terms and reference ranges when needed, thereby reducing data entry ambiguity, improving data quality, and supporting informed patient participation.
3.	IoT Agent (IA): autonomously manages real-time physiological signal acquisition, specifically 60-second Lead II ECG recordings, using wearable IoT devices. Collaborating with the Executor, the IA leverages LLM-driven conversational interfaces to guide patients in correctly using the ECG device, providing step-by-step instructions (e.g., electrode placement) and addressing errors based on the Executor’s real-time reports. The IA controls device operations: connecting, initiating recording, validating signal quality through preprocessing, and saving validated biosignals to a database without human intervention. The agent enhances reliability in ambient assisted living environments.
4.	Imaging Agent (IMA): is responsible for ingesting and organizing cardiac imaging data, rather than analyzing image content. It allows patients or clinicians to upload imaging files (for example, echocardiography or cardiac MRI in standard formats) together with basic metadata such as modality, study date, and body region performs simple format and integrity checks and stores these artifacts in secure storage linked to the patient’s longitudinal profile. In the current prototype, the IMA functions purely as an imaging data collector and registrar, while AI-based image analysis and feature extraction are reserved as future extensions of the system. In the current implementation, imaging data are primarily ingested as PDF-based clinical reports with structured upload forms capturing modality-specific parameters, and basic integrity checks are applied, including file-type validation, completeness verification, and consistency between declared metadata and file content.

	ECG Processing and Analysis
At the hardware level, the AD8232 acquisition module already applies an analog filtering chain that suppresses baseline drift and high-frequency noise, Consequently, the software pipeline focuses on validating and analyzing the resulting digital ECG signal rather than redoing low-level conditioning. After each recording, the IoT Agent invokes a validation tool that checks whether the uploaded trace matches the expected ECG format (sampling rate, duration, amplitude range) and basic quality criteria; segments affected by artifacts such as poor electrode contact or motion noise are rejected, and the patient is prompted to repeat the measurement.
For ECG segments that pass validation, the system relies on the open-source NeuroKit2 toolbox [19] to perform preprocessing, delineation, and feature extraction directly from the raw waveform. NeuroKit2 provides a well-established and widely used pipeline for biosignal analysis, allowing automated ECG cleaning, peak detection (P, Q, R, S, T), cardiac cycle delineation, heart-rate and heart-rate-variability estimation, and computation of additional morphological and quality indices for the specific features shown in Table 4. By leveraging this toolbox instead of reimplementing low-level signal processing, the framework reduces development and validation effort while obtaining a rich, clinically meaningful feature set that feeds the downstream decision-support components.

The AD8232 acquisition module performs the primary analog signal conditioning, including baseline drift reduction and high-frequency noise suppression. Consequently, the software pipeline focuses on validating the acquired ECG signal and extracting clinically relevant features rather than repeating low-level filtering.
After each recording, the IoT Agent invokes a dedicated ECG validation tool that verifies the recording format (sampling frequency, duration, and amplitude range) and assesses signal quality using the NeuroKit2 library [19]. Specifically, ECG quality is evaluated using the ho2025 method, which estimates beat-by-beat signal reliability based on the consistency of RR-interval detection between two independent QRS detectors. Recordings that fail the quality assessment are rejected, and the Executor requests a new acquisition before proceeding with downstream analysis.
For recordings that satisfy the quality criteria, NeuroKit2 performs ECG delineation and automatically extracts the temporal and morphological features required by the downstream cardiovascular risk assessment modules.

	Decision Support and Reasoning
	Interpretable Risk Scoring with Fuzzy Inference System (FIS)
Clinical risk scores such as Framingham, ASCVD, QRISK3, CHA₂DS₂-VASc, CHADS₂, and HAS-BLED are widely established tools derived from longitudinal cohort studies and incorporated into clinical guidelines. Their continued use in clinical practice is driven by parsimony, validated threshold structures, and direct clinical interpretability.

Rather than replacing these scores with black-box predictive models, the proposed framework augments them through a Fuzzy Inference System (FIS) operating on top of the canonical score definitions. This design preserves the original semantics, predictors, and categorical thresholds of each score while enabling:
●	graceful handling of uncertainty and missingness,
●	smooth transitions between adjacent risk categories,
●	factor-level explanations for why a patient falls into a given category, and
●	compact, patient-facing recommendations focused on modifiable risk factors.
In this way, the outputs remain aligned with familiar risk scores and guidelines but benefit from the flexibility and expressiveness of fuzzy reasoning.
	Fuzzy System Flow
The FIS pipeline (see Fig. 3) is applied uniformly across all implemented scores. Patient data collected by the agents are first normalized and mapped onto the corresponding predictors required by each score. When a required variable is temporarily unavailable (for example, HDL-cholesterol or systolic blood pressure variability), the system uses a bounded estimator constrained to clinically plausible ranges; these estimates are automatically replaced once actual measurements become available.
Continuous predictors are encoded using triangular or trapezoidal membership functions, while binary factors (such as diabetes or prior stroke) are represented with soft sets to accommodate documentation uncertainty and borderline cases. The qualitative logic of each score is formalized as a Mamdani rule base. For example, a prior stroke dominates the CHA₂DS₂‑VASc score, and systolic blood pressure ≥ 160 mmHg activates the hypertension term in HAS‑BLED.
Inference is performed using standard min–max aggregation followed by centroid defuzzification, yielding a continuous value in the native numerical range of each score. This value is then mapped to linguistic risk categories according to published cut-points, ensuring consistency with current clinical guidelines. Finally, the system generates a factor-wise narrative explanation indicating which antecedents fired salient rules and a set of recommendations targeted at modifiable contributors.
The proposed framework incorporates a Fuzzy Inference System (FIS) as a decision-support layer to improve the interpretation of established cardiovascular risk scores. Rather than replacing validated clinical scoring systems with a data-driven predictive model, the FIS operates on top of their original definitions, preserving the published predictors, thresholds, and clinical semantics while providing a continuous and more interpretable representation of cardiovascular risk.

Patient information collected by the agents is first mapped to the predictors required by the selected cardiovascular risk score. Continuous variables are represented using triangular or trapezoidal membership functions, while categorical clinical variables are modeled using fuzzy sets to accommodate uncertainty arising from incomplete or borderline patient information. A Mamdani inference engine with centroid defuzzification is then applied to generate a continuous risk estimate, which is subsequently mapped back to the corresponding linguistic risk category defined by the original scoring system. The resulting risk interpretation is provided to both patients and healthcare professionals together with concise explanations of the principal contributing factors and recommendations targeting modifiable risk factors. These outputs are intended solely to assist cardiovascular risk interpretation within the proposed IoMT workflow and should not be considered diagnostic decisions or replacements for clinical judgment. Clinical validation with real patient cohorts and healthcare professionals remains outside the scope of this work.

	Dashboard and Data Visualization
The framework exposes an integrated dashboard that consolidates multimodal patient data, analytical outputs, and risk assessments into a single interface, with tailored views for patient self-monitoring and clinician oversight. For patients, the dashboard displays core numerical health parameters as interactive time-series charts, alongside icon-based visualizations of lifestyle factors and comorbidities, and provides access to ECG results, including waveforms with detected peaks, annotations, and concise statistical summaries. Patients can browse historical sessions to track longitudinal evolution and are presented with the outputs of the FIS-based decision support, where each score is shown on its native scale together with linguistic categories (for example, “low,” “moderate,” or “high” risk) and short, patient-facing explanations focused on modifiable risk factors.
Clinicians access the same underlying information through a secure portal that emphasizes longitudinal trends, consolidated risk summaries, and pending tasks such as imaging requests or abnormal-findings notifications, while also providing channels for targeted messages to patients. By coupling role-specific visualization with consistent underlying data and risk models, the dashboard ensures that both patients and healthcare professionals interact with the system in a way that is transparent, actionable, and aligned with prevailing standards for clinical decision support and health information management.
	Security Considerations
As a research prototype, the proposed framework focuses on validating the agentic IoMT workflow rather than providing a production-ready medical platform. Nevertheless, several security measures were incorporated to mitigate common risks.
The backend enforces role-based access control (RBAC) to restrict access to patient records according to user privileges. Agent interactions are constrained through system prompts, structured tool inputs/outputs, and LangChain execution limits, reducing the risk of prompt injection, tool misuse, and unintended recursive tool-calling loops observed during early development. Communication between system components is intended to operate over secure channels (e.g., TLS in production deployments), while only the minimum patient information required for cardiovascular risk assessment is processed.
This prototype does not claim compliance with healthcare regulations such as HIPAA or GDPR. Advanced security mechanisms, including encrypted data storage, audit logging, consent management, comprehensive threat monitoring, and regulatory compliance, are considered future work toward clinical deployment.
